# The Inhabited Archive — Final Demo Script

Target: under 3:00 at a calm but purposeful pace. Bracketed directions and
captions are not spoken.

## 0:00–0:28 · On camera

Hi — I’m Shay Cranmer. I’m an educator, a historian, and a storyteller, and
this is The Inhabited Archive.

My students face two walls: language — you cannot engage with what is not
legible — and vastness: imagine thirty thousand books, knowing your answer is
somewhere inside.

I built this during OpenAI Build Week with Codex as an engineering partner. I
brought the scholarship and product judgment; Codex helped turn those values
into a working, testable instrument.

## 0:28–0:48 · On camera to screen

AI fits both walls — and can create new problems. So Codex and I defined
non-negotiables: never invent a source, hide uncertainty, confuse one shelf
with all of history, or take interpretation from the scholar. The result does
not hand over an answer. It returns passages worth your time.

## 0:48–1:18 · Screen: fox and table

**Caption: GPT‑5.6 proposes · the scholar approves**

The first librarian is the fox. I ask how distance changes what home means.
GPT‑5.6 helps clarify it, then returns a structured concept map instead of an
uncheckable paragraph.

Codex and I co-designed this through repeated scholarly walkthroughs. Testing
showed that people typed “yes” and expected the table to open, so we rewrote the
fox and the interface until the honest path was also the obvious one. Every card
remains editable. Nothing searches until I approve the table.

## 1:18–1:47 · Screen: badger folios

**Caption: Scholar-reviewed Latin guidance · every proposal inspectable**

The map goes to the Latin specialist. GPT‑5.6 drafts a badger folio for each
card: vocabulary, variants, language gaps, and expected false positives — all
editable before retrieval.

Codex turned a source-backed Latin method into strict schemas and tests. Other
AI reviewers attacked both method and code. One caught that we promised Latin
u/v-insensitive search before the index supported it. We fixed the pipeline,
added the test, and kept the receipt.

## 1:47–2:17 · Screen: shelf, receipt, and owl

**Caption: 30 complete Perseus works · 61,651 passages · exact citations**

Search is not left to the model. Application code applies my limits, searches
thirty complete Latin works, deduplicates overlaps, and preserves a receipt.

GPT‑5.6 powers the owl — but only over passages the corpus actually returned.
Codex built that evidence boundary. The owl explains each result, shows its
original-language context, labels machine translation as a reading aid, and
keeps doubtful passages visible — sometimes marked “probably not what you
need.”

## 2:17–2:43 · On camera

**Caption: Built with Codex · reviewed by multiple AIs · human approval at every gate**

I brought Codex messy scholarly notes, screenshots, questions, and confusion;
Codex translated them into architecture, code, tests, and explanations. I ran
genuine research questions, challenged what came back, and we revised together.
Other AIs served as adversarial reviewers; Codex and I evaluated their
objections instead of accepting them automatically. I approved every meaningful
change.

## 2:43–3:00 · On camera

The way we built it mirrors what it teaches: the model proposes, the corpus
provides evidence, and the human decides. I have spent my teaching life
matchmaking between person and subject. The Inhabited Archive scales that work.
The goal is not a better search engine. It is more people in relationship with
the Great Things.
