## Rejected And Transitional Directions

### Early triptych

Rejected because it used coloring-book rendering, boxed equal portrait
stations, round eyes and childlike proportions, pristine outlines without
engraved density, and Greek/Roman/Islamic architectural markers that assigned
each animal a tidy civilization.

Durable diagnosis:

> Competent, slightly strange manuscript creatures, not adorable app mascots.

### Ornamental vine version

It improved the marginalia-like page inhabitation and reduced rigid panels,
but the figures remained young and cute, civilizational shorthand remained,
and the result felt like decorative storybook art rather than adult scholarly
work.

### Final reference before the approved panorama

This version established adult fox, badger, and owl scholars; dense etched
linework; absorbed postures; parchment texture; and figures who looked
"mildly obsessive and capable of having opinions about variant readings."

It still required correction because it used three discrete portrait stations,
had too little interface-safe negative space, retained cultural visual mapping,
had too little of the pigment journey, and read as a cast introduction rather
than a modular website backing layer.

## Character Continuity

### Shared proportions and behavior

Keep:

- adult, naturalistic animal heads;
- relatively small eyes;
- long or species-appropriate muzzles;
- functional paws or wings engaged with scholarly tools;
- weight-bearing, absorbed working postures;
- a slender fox, stocky badger, and compact upright owl;
- attention directed toward the work rather than the viewer.

Avoid:

- oversized heads or baby bodies;
- giant expressive eyes;
- rounded plush-toy silhouettes;
- human smiles;
- performative gestures toward the viewer.

### Fox — query architect

The fox is slender and alert, with adult naturalistic proportions and a long,
attentive muzzle. The fox holds and compares query slips, works beside a small
card-catalog drawer, and sends rust-colored threads into a constellation of
connected cards. The fox communicates: *What do you mean, and what concepts
belong together?*

Continuity objects: catalog slips, drawers, linked notes, and thread
constellations.

### Badger — corpus walker

The badger is stockier and lower to the table, physically embedded in the
evidence. The badger hunches over several open codices, uses a magnifying lens,
and is surrounded by manuscripts, scrolls, loose leaves, and writing tools.
The badger communicates: *Let me dig through forms, variants, passages, and
context.*

Continuity objects: magnifying lens, multiple open codices, variants, scrolls,
and loose leaves.

### Owl — adjudicator

The owl is compact and upright, holding a candidate passage apart for
inspection. The owl has writing or marking tools nearby and works at a
lectern-like shelf surrounded by reference or evaluative instruments. The owl
communicates: *Does this passage actually mean something, or is it merely six
donkeys?*

Continuity objects: candidate passage, marking tool, lectern, provenance, and
evaluative instruments.

### Clothing

Use layered, plain scholarly garments held lightly rather than uniforms. They
should communicate "working scholar in an old textual world" without
identifying a culture, religion, language, or single historical period. The
owl's wrapped head covering and nearby lantern or architecture must never
become canon for an "Arabic owl."

## Recurrence And Personality

- Roles come first; personality emerges through repeated small behaviors.
- The librarians are recurring individuals, not a cast-of-characters gimmick.
- Sometimes show only tools, tracks, threads, feathers, leaves, or a partial
  edge crop.
- Use a full character when that mode of scholarly attention becomes active.
- The fox, badger, and owl are not language librarians and do not represent
  civilizations.
- Language specialists operate within the larger query-architecture,
  corpus-walking, and adjudication system.
