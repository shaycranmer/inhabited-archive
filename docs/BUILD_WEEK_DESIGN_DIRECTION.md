# The Inhabited Archive: Living Design Direction

Status: co-design in progress; preserve decisions without treating them as a
finished specification

Last updated: 2026-07-18

## Emotional North Star

The first encounter should feel like the world is opening. A scholar begins
with a question in a language she knows and discovers that the archive can
meet her with knowledgeable help across languages she does not yet read.

The interface should remain intellectually credible and source-auditable while
preserving part of what makes historical research pleasurable: atmosphere,
texture, strangeness, dark-academic delight, and the sense that the archive is
inhabited.

## Approved Public Identity

The public research instrument is named **The Inhabited Archive** with the
descriptor **A multilingual research instrument**. Number Rants remains the
underlying research project, proving ground, and demonstration inquiry. The
local directory and existing GitHub repository may retain their Number Rants
names until a later publication decision; the public product name does not
depend on renaming that infrastructure.

## Approved Opening Copy

Eyebrow:

> A multilingual research instrument

Headline:

> Translate the question.<br>
> Not the library.

Body:

> Ask in the language you know. Specialist librarians adapt your idea to the
> languages of the archive. They return the sources you should read and leave
> the interpretation with you.

The removed em dash is intentional. It had become an avoidable contemporary
AI-style tell.

## Approved Visual Rhythm

The public entry is one continuous page rather than a separate splash route.
The full librarian panorama is now the opening threshold itself: it carries
the approved headline and body in its central parchment while the fox,
badger, owl, cards, threads, architecture, and marginal growth inhabit the
edges. The earlier separate quiet hero and shorter “archive wakes” transition
were reviewed in the browser and deliberately merged. They made the panorama
feel small, repeated the invitation, and delayed the actual world opening.

The opening includes a clear `Bring a question to the fox` link. Clicking it
scrolls forward, while ordinary scrolling follows the same route. The sequence
is now:

1. illustrated opening panorama;
2. fox clarification room;
3. overhead concept worktable.

The rust-colored period after `question` remains as a small visual knot, but
the former CSS-only thread and index-card hero motif is superseded by the
threads and cards already living in the panorama.

The approved panorama file remains intact. The current page composition uses
two softly masked crops from that same source to test a fox-left and
badger/owl-right arrangement. This treatment successfully fills the side
gutters, but it also duplicates the badger because both display crops still
contain him. Treat the split as explicitly provisional and revisit it with
separate derived crops or assets; do not mistake the current treatment for an
approved final panorama. The master image itself remains untouched.

Each working scene should fit its primary heading, explanation, field, and
next action inside an ordinary laptop viewport whenever content length permits.
Spaciousness must not force the scholar to scroll merely to locate the action
belonging to the current scene.

## The Recurring Librarians

The characters are recurring individuals, but lightly held. They are not
mascots, named product guides, or one-to-one representations of languages or
civilizations. Their identities emerge from modes of scholarly attention:

- **Fox — query architect:** organizes catalog slips and threads conceptual
  connections; associated with the head-librarian conversation.
- **Badger — corpus walker:** digs into manuscripts, word forms, variants, and
  context; associated with retrieval and close inspection.
- **Owl — adjudicator and reading guide:** examines candidates, provenance,
  and whether a number receives qualitative meaning or merely counts six
  donkeys. For shortlisted source-language passages, the owl also supplies a
  clearly labeled provisional working translation and brief English
  orientation so the scholar can decide what deserves human reading time.
  Translation and relevance remain separate, inspectable claims.

Language-specific librarians may still exist within the architecture. Avoid
making an animal, costume, or monument stand in for Greek, Latin, Arabic,
Hebrew, or any other tradition.

Visual register: competent adult animal scholars rendered as manuscript
marginalia / fine early-print engraving. Historically strange and gently
whimsical, not baby-proportioned, coloring-book, cartoon, or generic fantasy
mascot art.

## Design Guardrails

- Atmosphere is part of the research invitation, not frivolous garnish.
- Scholarship and provenance establish credibility; the librarians establish
  welcome and make an invisible agent architecture graspable.
- Use the characters as inhabitants and stage markers, not constant stickers.
- Let motifs and partial crops carry continuity between full illustrations.
- Preserve negative space. The world should open rather than become crowded.
- Do not let the interface imply that the AI has answered the historical
  question. It helps the scholar discover what to read.

## Approved Working-Room Interaction

The first working room keeps conversation and the concept map visible on the
same responsive page. They are two interfaces to one shared inquiry, not a
chat followed by a separate card editor.

The fox first explains the method: before entering the library, scholar and
fox will decide what the question means, including concepts, relationships,
boundaries, and exclusions. The fox asks the minimum useful number of dynamic
clarifying questions, usually one to three, without requesting information the
scholar has already supplied. The scholar may move to the table whenever the
question has enough shape.

The public fox should feel warm, clever, attentive, and alive without becoming
a mascot performance. He begins in plain language by saying what he heard,
notices when several questions are tucked together, asks one clear question at
a time, and uses scholarly terminology only when it helps. Occasional dry
humor may arise naturally; forced whimsy, automatic praise, pet names,
flirting, and physical-affection gestures do not belong in public replies.

Small spaced all-caps labels are not acceptable when they impair legibility.
Fox-room labels and actions should use readable sizes, restrained spacing, and
strong contrast. Marginal linework, threads, foliate marks, and diagrammatic
ornament may wrap the writing field so it belongs to the room, but the field's
text and controls remain visually primary.

Fox response latency is explained through an honest catalogue-working
vignette rather than a fake percentage or generic spinner. During the first
map generation, small line-drawn catalogue cards shift beside plain-language
notes about organizing the inquiry. During revisions, the present table stays
visible and a compact note says that nothing will move until the fox returns.
The animation adds atmosphere but the text carries the status, and reduced-
motion preferences remain fully supported. No new raster art is required for
this state.

As they talk, the fox lays proposed cards onto a visible worktable. The scholar
may manipulate cards directly or continue the conversation when an issue needs
nuance. Either route changes the same underlying query map:

- `Keep`, `Edit`, and `Remove` are direct scholar actions.
- `Why this card?` asks the fox to explain its reasoning.
- `Needs your answer` is a distinct fox-authored state marking an unresolved
  question for the scholar.
- A scholar may also request any of these operations conversationally.
- The fox may clarify, ask for more information, and propose or alter cards in
  response.

The first overhead-table composition now uses the approved top-down desk art.
Search-concept families occupy the open center; the fox's latest note and the
same conversation sit beneath the illustrated paw; scope and active exclusions
remain smaller slips at the right; and the illustrated card pile anchors the
recoverable set-aside stack. Cards auto-arrange and can be reordered by drag or
keyboard-accessible controls. Their visual order has no retrieval effect.

Relationships are explicit query-graph records surfaced as labeled rust
strings attached directly to their source concept cards, with the destination
named beside the string. They are not a separate section above the map, are
never inferred from card position, and the fox need not connect every card.
The scholar may edit, pin, add, or remove a relationship string. The table ends
with a clear scholar approval action and an equally visible route to keep
working with the fox.
Approval now enters the installed language-specialist folio desk and stops
honestly after inspectable language adaptation; it does not pretend that
retrieval has run. The current installed demonstration language is Latin.

The system should not require the scholar to approve every reasonable card
individually. The scholar edits what matters, then approves the map as a whole.
Before search begins, the fox summarizes the resulting search contract in one
plain-language sentence. The language and corpus librarians enter only after
the scholar confirms that this contract represents the inquiry.

Scope choices and active exclusions are fully scholar-editable. Either may be
edited and pinned, added directly, set aside recoverably, or explicitly moved
to the other category. Moving an exclusion back to scope visibly means that
matching material is allowed into consideration again; the system must not
make that semantic change silently.

The documented no-key example deliberately uses the accessible question of
how distance changes writers' ideas of home. It demonstrates the instrument
without requiring a visitor to enter midway through Shay's specialized Number
Rants argument. Number Rants remains the proving ground and may still provide
the eventual live demonstration, but is not baked into the fox's generic query
grammar.

Technical model: both panes operate on one shared, versioned query graph.
Conversation produces proposed graph mutations; card interactions produce
structured mutations. Human and agent actions must remain attributable and
undoable.

## Approved Card Authority And Memory

The fox may refine its own unreviewed suggestions while the clarification
conversation is still unfolding. The moment the scholar keeps, edits, creates,
or pins a card, that card becomes scholar-controlled. The fox may question it
or propose a visible revision, but may not silently overwrite it. Direct
conversational permission such as “revise those cards” authorizes only the
named change.

No proposed idea is permanently deleted. Removing a card moves it into a
visible **set-aside stack** at the edge of the worktable. The stack shows a
count and opens into a browsable drawer where the scholar may inspect, restore,
or discuss earlier cards. Restored cards retain their origin and change
history.

Set-aside cards remain available as conversational memory so the fox does not
casually re-suggest an idea the scholar already declined. If later changes make
one newly relevant, the fox may ask whether to restore it but may not return it
to the active map automatically.

An inactive card and an exclusion card are different:

- **Set aside** means “do not use this idea in the current query.” It has no
  retrieval effect.
- **Exclusion** means “actively deprioritize or reject results matching this
  pattern,” such as dates, chapter numbers, or incidental quantities.

The interface may affectionately render the set-aside stack as a discard pile,
but the user-facing action should say `Set aside` rather than imply permanent
destruction. All mutations remain undoable.

## Approved Research And Product Scope

Number Rants is the proving ground, not the permanent topical boundary of the
instrument. The reusable product accepts an arbitrary scholarly question and
helps the scholar construct, adapt, and run an inspectable search against the
corpora and language specialists actually installed.

The honest boundary is **arbitrary questions over a bounded, declared
library**. The application must never hardcode its concept grammar, fox
conversation, retrieval terms, or source ranking around the eventual video
demonstration. A scripted question may teach or demonstrate the method, but a
researcher running the application with an API key must be able to enter a
different question and receive a dynamically constructed inquiry.

The fox may recognize that a question falls outside current corpus coverage
and offer historically meaningful conceptual bridges without pretending the
archive contains what it does not. For example, a question about radios could
be redirected toward historically available questions about sound,
communication, acoustics, or transmission. The scholar decides whether any
bridge remains her question.

The deeper Number Rants research hunch includes the possibility that some
qualitative number discourse encodes personal cognition, including sensory or
synesthetic organization, which then becomes public pedagogy. The project is
therefore interested both in inherited traditions and in changes where an
author's distinctive cognition appears to reorganize or overwhelm what was
received. Retrieval should surface the comparative evidence needed to study
that possibility; it must not diagnose historical authors or claim that
synesthesia has been proved.

## Approved Visible Card Grammar

The worktable has four distinct intellectual territories:

- **Search concepts** dominate the central table. These are the ideas the
  corpus would search if it could be searched intelligently in the scholar's
  working language.
- **Scope choices** remain visible and editable but occupy a quieter secondary
  area. They may include dates, traditions, languages, corpora, genres, or
  other boundaries.
- **Active exclusions** remain visible and editable in their own secondary
  area. They mean "do not bring me results matching this pattern" and have an
  active retrieval effect.
- **Set-aside ideas** occupy a visible, recoverable discard stack. They mean
  "not part of the present focus" and have no retrieval effect.

Pinning, fox authorship, scholar authorship or editing, unresolved state, and
language adaptation are card states rather than additional intellectual
types.

A search-concept card may represent a **concept family**, not merely one
keyword. A family such as `Titles of Christ` can contain Jesus, Messiah,
Logos, Lamb, Son of Man, and other child terms without flooding the table with
top-level cards. The collapsed card previews representative members and a
count. The expanded card exposes every member, its rationale, and its state.
The scholar may edit, pin, or set aside individual family members directly. A
child becomes its own top-level card only when it needs independent reasoning,
weighting, scope, or discussion.

Opening a family expands it in place while the surrounding worktable gently
recedes without disappearing. The scholar remains at the same table rather
than entering a separate editor.

## Approved Focus Of Inquiry

The scholar may mark exactly one concept family or one child concept as the
`Focus of Inquiry`, or may leave the inquiry broad. Focus is a retrieval and
reasoning priority: it tells the fox and later librarians which idea should
lead while other cards remain available as context, comparison, or
complication. It is not inferred from card position, card expansion, or visual
proximity.

Focus and pinning are independent. `Pin` protects scholar-controlled material
from silent fox revision; `Focus of Inquiry` says what matters most. Choosing a
new focus visibly transfers the designation. The focused family or child has
an explicit text label plus restrained lapis/teal support, so the state never
depends on color alone. The fox asks before table approval whether the scholar
wants to name a focus or keep the inquiry broad.

Card language must be immediately judgeable, not merely interpretable after
effort. Labels should usually name the concrete search choice in ordinary
language. Rationales should first say what the search will include, emphasize,
or keep out, then add methodological nuance. For example, prefer `Search across
multiple regions and traditions` plus `Keep the search broad for now; narrow
it after the evidence shows meaningful differences` over compressed phrasing
such as `Comparative premodern breadth`.

## Approved Card-Scoped Conversation

An expanded card contains a compact `Talk to the fox about this card` field.
This is a narrowed aperture into the original fox conversation, not a new
agent or a separate chat. The resulting exchange remains in the full
conversation and carries a visible context marker such as `Regarding: Titles
of Christ`.

Explicit scoped permission such as "keep Logos and Lamb; set the rest aside"
may produce the named changes. Ambiguous requests produce visible proposals.
Pinned scholar-controlled material is not silently overridden. Every change
leaves a compact receipt and an undo path inside the card; the fuller reply
remains in the shared conversation.

## Approved Restoration Behavior

Restoration must never disappear into application state without a visible
destination. A set-aside child offers at least:

- `Restore to original family: Titles of Christ`, preserving its origin and
  history; and
- `Make new table card`, when the scholar now wants independent treatment.

After restoration the destination updates immediately, the restored item is
temporarily marked, and the interface provides `View card` and `Undo`. A
restrained thread or pigment movement may support the transition, but text,
focus management, and state changes must communicate it without color or
motion. If the original family is also set aside, the scholar may restore the
family with the child or create a new top-level card.

## Approved Atmosphere And Visual Semantics

The idealized texture of archival research is load-bearing interaction
design, not an "old-timey" theme. Dust, paper, linework, threads, marginal
inhabitants, and accumulated pigment invite patience, curiosity, inspection,
and tolerance for ambiguity. They should help machine assistance deepen the
scholar's attention rather than flatten research into answer extraction.

Cards may carry restrained manuscript or early-print linework at their edges
so they belong to the same inhabited archive as the librarians. Teal and
lapis can enter card details as the map becomes multilingual.

Owl-adjudicated source rankings may become more visually illuminated as
relational evidence strengthens. Because hue already explains the warm-to-
multilingual journey, relevance should be expressed through pigment density,
ornamental accumulation, ordering, labels, confidence, and plain-language
reasons rather than a secret color code. The visual layer supports the
evidence and remains dispensable to understanding it.

## Approved Portable Demonstration Boundary

The Project Day artifact should ship or obtain a compact, publication-safe
micro-corpus broad enough for retrieval to succeed, fail, and surprise. A
developer supplies her own API key for open-ended fox and librarian behavior.
The full experience must search the real installed index rather than select
from a pre-seeded answer packet.

The existing three-passage packet remains useful only as a regression fixture,
negative-control demonstration, or clearly labeled no-key tour. It is not the
main evidence that generic retrieval works. The eventual video may follow a
carefully scripted path, but the application beneath it remains topic-general.

The portable retrieval shelf is now fixed at 30 complete Latin works from a
pinned Perseus `canonical-latinLit` snapshot. It is balanced across five
declared genre/domain baskets and was selected before the eventual video
question. See `docs/DEMO_LATIN_CORPUS.md` and
`sources/indexes/demo_latin_30.csv`. The local manifest-driven SQLite corpus
index, D1 serving projection, diagnostic lexical checks, approved-plan
retrieval, and owl handoff are now built and locally verified.

The owl room's current composition is deliberately functional rather than
visually ratified. It makes the connected run history, immutable receipt,
source-grounded reading order, exact original text, English orientation,
machine-translation caveat, confidence, and reasons visible. Strong and
possible results receive automatic translation only when the scholar selects
that policy; weaker translations are available on demand; translation can be
disabled for the run. A later folio revision creates a visibly connected child
run rather than replacing the old list.

## Still Open

The opening, topic-general clarification room, and overhead worktable now have
first integrated compositions. At compact laptop and in-app-browser widths,
the fox art and conversation remain side by side; they stack only on narrow
phone layouts. The table composition has passed a first browser inspection at
the compact in-app width but remains a co-design prototype awaiting Shay's
review rather than an approved finished layout.

The next co-design boundary is a complete visual-language pass across the real
Latin journey, especially the transition from approved folios through archive
retrieval into owl waiting and ranked results. Still-open decisions include the
owl's final visual grammar, fox clarification rhythm, corpus-coverage bridge
behavior, character micro-behaviors, animation restraint, responsive crop
refinement, durable run export/storage, and the video path.

Language and corpus adaptations must be inspectable before retrieval. Each fox
concept family becomes one collapsed badger folio rather than an explosion of
top-level cards. Opening the folio exposes individually editable, pinnable, and
deactivatable lexical, morphological, historical-semantic, conceptual,
exclusion, and uncertainty proposals. The collapsed state summarizes direct
and exploratory counts, scope, highest risk, confidence, edit/approval state,
and how many proposals have been previewed.

Every searchable proposal may offer an on-demand **Check literal coverage on
this shelf** control. It reports actual match and work counts for that
proposal's forms alone; it does not predict the result count for the whole
inquiry. Raw citable contexts from different works remain collapsed behind an
explicit note that they are most useful to a reader of the source language. It
is a catalogue check, not owl adjudication: it does not combine the approved
concept map, rank relevance, approve or mutate the plan, or turn zero shelf
hits into a claim of historical absence. Verification
language must distinguish packet-guided model proposal, corpus attestation,
and genuine external lexical verification. The underlying v1 contract and
local deterministic preview are implemented. The live Latin route now also
enforces explicit fox-table approval, exact source-card identity, and
application-owned verification labels. Three unrelated live plans initially
took about two minutes apiece and returned roughly 13–18 proposals across two
families; a later full folio handoff took closer to five minutes, so the badger
room needs an honest waiting state and must make collapsed folios
the default. That first composition is now implemented: a quiet language desk,
an honest rotating wait notice, compact folio covers, in-place expansion,
recoverable proposal set-aside, proposal editing and pins, and per-folio
approval. Approved folios recollapse and clearly offer reopening for
inspection. Dedicated badger art now enters from the upper-left behind the
room, and the fox worktable art fades into uninterrupted parchment rather than
ending at a hard illustrated boundary. The local runtime bridge now completes
the Latin path, but the room remains a co-design implementation awaiting a
focused visual review rather than a frozen finished composition. Public D1,
rate-limit verification, and deployment remain open.
